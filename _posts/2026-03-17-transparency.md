*I created this essay for the introductory course for PhD students at MIT. This was my first experience interviewing someone with a different epistemic background. I tried to make him reflect on the concepts of transparency and replicability. This was hard and made me realize that there are many things so obvious to me that are not obvious to other people in the Data Visualization field*


The scientific community increasingly demands transThe scientific community increasingly demands transparency, and this appears to be a fully legitimate demand. We should be able to scrutinize and audit what scientists are working on and how they do so in order to increase the trustworthiness and rigor of research. Transparency allows us to understand who is behind the research, what decisions were made, what was included, and what was excluded, among many other aspects that shape the production of knowledge. However, transparency often seems difficult to both encourage and achieve.
For this reason, developing a better understanding of how different subfields of data visualization understand transparency, research rigor, and the barriers and opportunities associated with these practices is fundamental for the scientific community. In this essay, I reflect on the opportunities and challenges for the Computer Graphics and Image Processing Unit and the Visualization and Interaction Design Unit in achieving transparency. In particular, I compare my own research perspective with insights from another unit within the division.
The Media and Information Technology Division is a research community with rich and diverse epistemic foundations and approaches. Therefore, our understandings of what transparency is and how to achieve it vary from unit to unit and even within the same unit. Instead of seeing this diversity as a problem, I would argue that it represents an opportunity for the Media and Information Technology Division. Different perspectives allow us to learn from one another and potentially develop shared principles or guidelines that strengthen and improve the rigor of research practices across the division.
My PhD project explores how the research process—which is often messy, emotional, and non-linear—can be documented and communicated in ways that go beyond traditional academic formats such as papers or conference presentations. My work seeks to make visible not only the decisions made during the research process, but also the uncertainties, failures, and experiences that shape it. These elements are often hidden behind the clean and linear narrative of academic publications, even though they are an important part of how knowledge is actually produced.
Within the variety of approaches and epistemic foundations that coexist in the Media and Information Technology Division, the two units that I focus on in this essay occupy quite different positions. From my conversations with the unit heads, I could see that the Computer Graphics and Image Processing Unit has a much stronger technical component and is rooted in a more empirical, positivist research tradition. In contrast, the Visualization and Interaction Design Unit mostly employs methods of inquiry that include subjective and reflective practices, such as design methods.
Given the nature of the work carried out in each unit, it is not surprising that their understanding of transparency also differs. For this reason, in the following section, I will briefly describe some of the research conducted within these units and explore how their practices relate to the idea of transparency.
The Computer Graphics and Image Processing Unit mainly develops computational methods. On the one hand, they focus on developing techniques, such as models and algorithms, to accurately represent reality visually. In addition, they develop machine learning algorithms that process large amounts of visual data to generate new methods of analysis and representation. The research outcomes are usually algorithms, code, and experimental results, which are mostly presented through tables. There are no strict guidelines for achieving transparency in academic articles; however, reproducibility of experiments is at the core of transparency. This often involves sharing the code and data used in order to reproduce the results.
Meanwhile, the Visualization and Interaction Design Unit focuses on exploring new ways in which people interact with data, technology, and digital materials. Their research is often situated, contextual, and critical. As a result, the methodologies they employ often involve design methods and qualitative, subjective approaches that are iterative and non-linear. In this context, reproducibility and replicability are more difficult to achieve, since knowledge is constructed in situated and contextual ways. Transparency in this type of research is therefore understood differently. Rather than focusing primarily on replicating results, transparency often involves exposing the artifacts produced throughout the research process, such as field notes, prototypes, sketches, and other materials that trace the research process from a material perspective.
Although achieving some level of reproducibility may seem straightforward within the Computer Graphics and Image Processing Unit, it is not exempt from difficulties. In my conversation with the unit head, he mentioned how challenging it can be to reproduce their own work. For example, datasets may no longer be available, the data used may require very large computational resources, or many parameters and configurations may not be fully documented. Therefore, even though reproducibility may appear obvious or relatively simple, in practice, it is not so easy to achieve. This also shows that transparency cannot be reduced to technical reproducibility alone, since many decisions and infrastructural factors shape what can actually be reproduced.
Similarly, transparency in design-oriented research processes poses significant challenges. Transparency cannot be reduced simply to showing the research process through the artifacts produced, as these materials can sometimes be difficult to interpret or insufficient for transferring knowledge. Sketches, prototypes, or field notes may document parts of the process, but without the accompanying reflections and decisions behind them, they can become fragmented or illegible to others. For this reason, the challenges of transparency within the Visualization and Interaction Design Unit are not focused solely on reproducibility or replication, but rather on making the construction of knowledge legible through our research processes.
These different barriers can also be understood as opportunities. As mentioned earlier, those of us working with design-oriented research processes have often spent more time questioning how evidence is produced in order to generate and transfer knowledge. As a result, our practices tend to involve a high degree of reflexivity and awareness regarding the decisions made throughout the research process. One anecdotal moment that I found particularly revealing of these different perspectives occurred at the end of my conversation with the head of the Computer Graphics and Image Processing Unit. After reflecting on questions about documentation and research practices, he jokingly commented that the conversation had felt “like therapy,” adding that he rarely takes the time to step back and reflect on his own research practices in that way. For those of us working within more interpretative or design-oriented traditions, this kind of reflection is often part of our everyday research practice and therefore does not feel unusual.
Moments like this highlight how reflexivity itself can contribute to improving transparency in research. For example, it may help researchers become more aware of the decisions made during the research process and improve the documentation and communication of complex parameters and configurations in more technical research contexts. In this sense, a more reflective approach to research practices could foster greater awareness of methodological choices and facilitate the reporting of methods and results. At the same time, improving transparency may open opportunities beyond research reporting and even generate new forms of knowledge, while also making the researcher’s position visible in relation to the research. This position is inevitably connected to the decisions about what is included in the process and what is excluded, as well as the reasons behind those choices.
In conclusion, beyond the epistemic differences within the Media and Information Technology Division, transparency can be understood both as a responsibility and an opportunity. Research perspectives that encourage greater reflection on research processes can not only improve transparency but also help us better understand and make sense of what we are doing as researchers. Ultimately, this can lead to more rigorous and generative research practices. Reflecting on how our research is produced and how it relates to the world around us may therefore represent a valuable opportunity for dialogue across the different units within the Media and Information Technology Division.
